## 1. The CIA Triad

The three core principles of cybersecurity:

- **Confidentiality** — protecting sensitive information from unauthorized access
  through encryption, access controls, and data classification
- **Integrity** — ensuring data is accurate and has not been tampered with
  through checksums, digital signatures, and version control
- **Availability** — making sure systems are accessible when needed
  through redundancy, backups, and disaster recovery planning

---

## 2. Threat Landscape

- **Threat Actors** — who attacks systems:
  - Script kiddies (low skill, use existing tools)
  - Cybercriminals (money motivated)
  - Nation-state actors (government backed, most advanced)
- **Attack Vectors** — how attackers get in:
  - Technical exploits (software vulnerabilities)
  - Social engineering (manipulating people)
- **Vulnerability Management** — finding, assessing, and fixing security weaknesses

---

## 3. Security Technologies and Controls

- **Authentication and Authorization** — verifying who you are and what you can access
  - Multi-factor authentication (MFA) — something you know + something you have
  - Access control systems — only give users what they need
- **Encryption and Cryptography**
  - Symmetric encryption — same key to encrypt and decrypt
  - Asymmetric encryption — public key to encrypt, private key to decrypt
  - Hashing — converts data into a fixed string, cannot be reversed
  - Digital certificates — prove identity online
- **Security Frameworks** — industry standards that guide how organisations build security programs

---

## 4. Web Security

- **Surface Web** — what you access through normal browsers (Google, websites)
- **Deep Web** — content not indexed by search engines (email inboxes, banking portals)
- **Dark Web** — requires special software like Tor, used for both legal privacy and illegal activity
- **Web Application Security** — protecting websites from common vulnerabilities
- **Digital Forensics Basics** — how evidence is collected and analyzed after a cybersecurity incident

---

## 5. Cybersecurity Awareness

### Digital Footprint
- Everything you do online leaves a trace
- Understand what digital assets you have and their value to attackers
- Limit personal information shared across digital platforms
- Social media is a major source of information for attackers

### Social Engineering
Attackers exploit human psychology rather than technical systems

- **RASCALS Framework** — manipulation techniques attackers use:
  - **R** — Reciprocation (I did something for you, now you owe me)
  - **A** — Authority (I am your IT department, give me your password)
  - **S** — Social Proof (everyone else is doing it)
  - **C** — Commitment (you already agreed to this)
  - **A** — Liking (attackers build rapport before attacking)
  - **L** — (repeat of above — framework variation)
  - **S** — Scarcity (act now or lose access forever)

### Threat Recognition
- **Phishing** — fake emails or messages designed to steal credentials or install malware
- **Malware** — malicious software including viruses, ransomware, spyware, trojans
- **Physical Security threats** — shoulder surfing, tailgating, device theft

---

## 6. Operational Security (OPSEC)

OPSEC means thinking proactively about protecting information before an attack happens.

- **Information Protection** — small pieces of information combined can reveal sensitive details
- **Threat Modeling** — identify your assets, who wants them, and how they might get them
- **Digital Hygiene** — consistent security habits across all digital activity
- **Communication Security** — securing conversations, emails, and information sharing
- **Information Classification** — categorizing data by sensitivity level
- **Security Culture** — security is everyone's responsibility, not just the IT department
- **Incident Response Awareness** — knowing your role when a security incident occurs

---

## Key Principles to Remember

- Think like an attacker — understanding how systems work helps you find weaknesses
- Security is a process, not a product — the threat landscape constantly evolves
- People are part of the system — human psychology is often the weakest link
- Balance is key — security must be practical or people will work around it
- Context matters — security decisions must match your specific risk profile
